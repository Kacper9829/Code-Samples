Chinese Language

Uses characters (汉字, hànzì) each representing a syllable and a meaning. Characters aren’t alphabetic; they’re logographic. You memorize shapes, not spelling rules. To help learners, there’s Pinyin: a Romanization system (e.g., zhōngguó = China).

No verb conjugations: Verbs don’t change for tense, person, or number. chī = eat / ate / will eat (context tells you which). No articles: No “a” or “the”. No cases or gender: Nouns don’t change form Plurals: Usually same as singular (苹果 = apple/apples).

Words are often short and combined into compounds:电话 (diànhuà = electric + speech = telephone). 电脑 (diànnǎo = electric + brain = computer). Modern Chinese borrows from English, especially tech:咖啡 (kāfēi = coffee), 沙发 (shāfā = sofa). Many homophones (same sound, different meaning) because there are relatively few syllables—tones + context are key.
